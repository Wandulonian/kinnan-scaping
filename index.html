🏡 Landscaping in Omaha

Landscaping in Omaha, Papillion, Gretna & Elkhorn: What Homeowners Should Know

If you own a home in the Omaha metro area, finding the right landscaper can make or break your property. Here's what we learned.

Nebraska Weather Isn't Kind to Cheap Work

Between the freeze-thaw cycles in Omaha winters and the heavy clay soil common across Papillion, Gretna, and Elkhorn, landscaping here takes real punishment. A retaining wall that isn't built with proper drainage and footer depth won't last two seasons — and neither will your driveway if heavy equipment crosses it before the concrete cures.

The Retaining Wall Trap

Retaining walls are one of the most common projects in the Omaha metro area — everyone wants to level their backyard. But they're also one of the most commonly botched jobs. Poor drainage behind the wall, inadequate base material, and undersized block selection are the usual culprits. A wall built wrong won't fail quietly — it'll bulge, crack, and eventually collapse. And by then, the landscaper is long gone.

Protect Your Driveway — Seriously

Here's the thing nobody tells you: a brand new concrete driveway in Papillion needs a minimum of 7 days to cure before heavy equipment should drive on it. Many contractors will tell you it's fine sooner — especially if accessing the backyard is easier that way. Don't take their word for it. Get the curing timeline in writing, and if they need to access the backyard, make them lay down plywood or track mats. A few minutes of prep now can save you thousands in concrete repair later.

If a landscaper insists on driving heavy equipment across fresh concrete without protection, that's a red flag — not an inconvenience.

What We'd Do Differently

Looking back, we should have been more involved during the first day of work. By the time we saw the CAT skid loader on our new driveway in Papillion, the damage was already done. Be present on day one. Walk the property before they start. Set clear boundaries in writing. We live and learn, but you don't have to.

🔍 Homeowner Tips

How to Vet a Landscaper in the Omaha Metro Area

After our experience, here's the screening process we wish we'd followed before hiring anyone for landscaping work in Omaha, Papillion, Gretna, or Elkhorn.

1. Get Three Bids — And Read Them Carefully

Price isn't everything, but the cheapest bid in Omaha often is for a reason. Compare not just the total but what's included: base preparation, drainage, materials, clean-up, and warranty. A bid that skips details now will skip more later.

2. Verify Insurance Directly

Don't take their word for it. Ask for a certificate of insurance and call the agency to confirm it's active. Ask specifically whether their policy covers damage to existing structures (driveways, patios, walkways) — not just liability for injuries. Many policies exclude damage caused by heavy equipment on surfaces not designed for it.

3. Check Their Online Presence

Search their name plus "complaint," "lawsuit," and "review" — and read the negative reviews first. Check court records in Sarpy and Douglas County. A business that's been sued before is a business that hasn't fixed its processes. Also check their social media — if all their photos are renderings and stock imagery, that's a warning sign.

4. Get Everything in Writing

Every promise, every timeline, every material specification. If they say they won't drive on your driveway, get it in the contract. If they promise to protect surfaces, get it in writing. Verbal agreements disappear the moment something goes wrong — and in a lawsuit, they might as well have never existed.

5. Never Pay 100% Upfront

A deposit of 25-50% is standard in the landscaping industry. Anything more, walk away. Once they have all your money, you have no leverage — and if they damage your property, they know you can't withhold payment as leverage either. In our case, even a 50% deposit didn't protect us from being sued for the rest.

6. Check for Liens and License

In Nebraska, you can check with the Nebraska Secretary of State to see if a business is in good standing. Also search county court records for mechanic's liens filed by suppliers or subcontractors — if a landscaper isn't paying their material bills, those liens can end up on your property, even if you've already paid the contractor in full.
